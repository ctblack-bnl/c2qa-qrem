## ⚠ No consistent cross-material relationship between BCS-derived gap and film thickness; finding is confounded by deterministic derivation from Tc and dominated by material identity effects
**Hypothesis key:** `derived_BCS_gap_meV_vs_film_thickness_nm`  
**Finding type:** derived_field_artifact | **Confidence:** 0.15 | **Phase B assessment:** unsupported

Across 43 samples spanning nine material systems, no coherent directional relationship between film thickness and the BCS-derived superconducting gap is observed. The only reproducible thickness dependence is within the NbSe2 ultrathin series (Zaman_2025, 1.99–10.07 nm), where gap increases with thickness due to well-known 2D quantum confinement effects — a material-class-specific phenomenon, not a general thin-film result. Critically, the derived_BCS_gap_meV field is computed deterministically from Tc_K (Δ = 1.764·k_B·Tc), meaning this hypothesis is mathematically identical to asking whether Tc correlates with film thickness, and no independent gap information is present in the corpus.

**Detail:** The derived_BCS_gap_meV field carries zero independent information beyond Tc_K, as it is computed via a fixed algebraic transformation (Δ = 1.764·k_B·Tc). Any apparent gap–thickness correlation is therefore identically a Tc–thickness hypothesis, and the two cannot be distinguished with this corpus. Within the NbSe2 series, the positive gap–thickness relationship (D1: 0.532 meV at 1.99 nm; D2: 0.730 meV at 4.08 nm; D5/D8: 1.064 meV at ~8–10 nm with apparent saturation) reflects the well-documented disorder- and quantum-confinement-driven Tc suppression in the ultrathin 2D limit — a phenomenon specific to van der Waals materials and not generalizable to conventional metallic thin films. Across all other materials, gap magnitude is overwhelmingly determined by material identity: NbN at 5 nm (Wu_2026, 1.520 meV) and 20 nm (Böttcher_2025, 1.824 meV) both exceed NbSe2 at comparable thicknesses (~1.06 meV), while PtSi at 25 nm (0.137 meV) and Re at 150 nm (0.289 meV) are far lower than Ta at 100–200 nm (0.638–0.669 meV). Within Ta, comparing Bland_2025 (200 nm, 0.638 meV) with Potluri_2025 Row 1 (100 nm, 0.669 meV) shows a slightly higher gap at lower thickness, opposite to the seeding hypothesis direction, but the difference is small and confounded by substrate differences (Si vs. Al₂O₃). The heavy clustering of samples at fixed thicknesses within each study means the dataset lacks the within-material thickness variation needed to test this hypothesis statistically.

**Evidence:** 43 samples | 1 author-stated | Materials: Re, Re (with Al/AlOx junction), Al, Ta-Hf (83:17), PtSi, NbSe2, Ta (with Al/AlOx junction), NbN, Ta

**Supporting:** Within the NbSe2 series (Zaman_2025 D1–D8), gap increases monotonically from ~0.53 meV at 1.99 nm to ~1.06 meV at ~8–10 nm, consistent with thickness-dependent Tc suppression in atomically thin 2D materials; this is the only genuine thickness sweep in the corpus.

**Complicating:** All other material groups show no thickness variation: Al samples are clustered at 200 nm, Re at 150 nm, PtSi at 25 nm, and Ta-Hf at 250/470 nm with essentially identical gaps within each group. The single author-stated correlation seed (Chang_2025) involves Au-encapsulation proximity-effect suppression of Ta — a fundamentally different physical mechanism from intrinsic film thickness effects — and should not be generalized.

**Alternative explanations:** Material identity (bulk Tc, crystalline phase, disorder level) is the dominant driver of gap magnitude across this corpus, completely overwhelming any thickness signal in cross-material comparisons. The seeding correlation from Chang_2025 reflects proximity-effect suppression by a Au normal-metal overlayer — a distinct mechanism from intrinsic thickness-dependent Tc — and its inclusion as a seed for a general thickness–gap hypothesis is physically unjustified.

**QREM implications:** A universal QREM mapping function of the form Δ = f(thickness) is not supported by this corpus and should not be implemented. The finding indicates that any thickness-to-gap mapping in QREM must be (a) material-specific, not cross-material, and (b) grounded in directly measured gap values rather than Tc-derived estimates. For the NbSe2 material class specifically, a positive monotonic mapping with saturation above ~8 nm is plausible and could be parameterized from the Zaman_2025 series, but this is a narrow, 2D-material-specific function. For all other materials in this corpus (Al, Ta, NbN, Re, PtSi, Ta-Hf), the current data provide no basis for a thickness-dependent gap mapping; material identity and substrate/deposition conditions should be the primary input variables in the QREM mapping layer. The derived-field issue means QREM should flag any gap values sourced from BCS derivation as non-independent from Tc, and the mapping layer should not treat them as separate observables.

**Per-material recommendation:** Yes, this hypothesis should be re-run per material class, but only NbSe2 currently has sufficient within-material thickness variation (8 samples across 1.99–10.07 nm) to support a focused re-analysis. NbN has two thickness points (5 nm and 20 nm from different studies) that could support a limited two-point comparison but not a robust regression. All other materials (Al, Ta, Re, PtSi, Ta-Hf) are clustered at one or two fixed thicknesses and cannot support within-material thickness analysis with current data. Ta is the highest-priority material for targeted data collection given its prevalence in quantum computing and the availability of two thickness points (100 nm and 200 nm) that hint at a possible relationship but are confounded by substrate differences.

**Suggested follow-up:** The highest-value next step is to identify or commission a systematic thickness series for a single material (Ta or NbN are the most quantum-computing-relevant) on a single substrate with identical deposition conditions, spanning at least 5–10 thickness values from ~10 nm to ~300 nm, with both Tc measurements and direct tunneling spectroscopy gap measurements. This would simultaneously resolve the Tc–thickness question, provide independent gap data not derived from Tc, and establish whether the NbSe2 result generalizes to conventional metallic superconductors. As a lower-cost alternative, a meta-analysis of existing literature Tc vs. thickness data for Ta and NbN (which have extensive published datasets) could be conducted to test the hypothesis without new experiments.

## ✗ No consistent cross-material relationship between deposition temperature and Tc is observed; material identity dominates, with only a weak, non-monotonic within-alloy trend in Ta-Hf (83:17).
**Hypothesis key:** `Tc_K_vs_deposition_temperature_C`  
**Finding type:** negative | **Confidence:** 0.25 | **Phase B assessment:** inconclusive

Across 28 samples spanning six material systems, no consistent directional relationship between deposition temperature and Tc is detectable at the cross-material level; the ordering of Tc by material (NbN ~12 K >> Ta-Hf ~6 K > Ta ~4.3 K >> Re ~1.9 K > Al ~1.1–1.4 K) bears no relationship to their respective deposition temperatures. Within the Ta-Hf (83:17) alloy system (Yang_2026, 8 samples, 550–850°C), a weak negative trend is present (~0.37 K over 300°C) that is directionally consistent with the seeding hypothesis, but it is non-monotonic and small in magnitude. This finding is informative as a negative result: it confirms that material selection is the primary lever for Tc optimization, and deposition temperature is at best a second-order, material-specific correction.

**Detail:** The cross-material comparison is dominated by intrinsic material properties rather than deposition temperature: NbN (Tc ~12 K, deposited at 400°C) vastly outperforms Re (Tc ~1.9 K, deposited at 900°C), and this ordering reflects bulk electron-phonon coupling differences, not a deposition temperature effect. For Ta, three data points spanning 22°C (Potluri_2025 Row 1, Tc = 4.4 K), 500°C (Potluri_2025 Row 2, Tc = 4.3 K), and 625°C (Bland_2025, Tc = 4.2 K) show negligible Tc variation over a 600°C range, providing strong within-material evidence against a general deposition-temperature–Tc relationship. For Al (Hedrick_2026), the apparent Tc difference between cryo deposition (−72°C, Tc = 1.15 K) and warm deposition (200°C, Tc = 1.05 K) is better attributed to transport regime (dirty vs. clean limit driven by grain boundary density) than to deposition temperature as a direct cause. The only material-specific signal consistent with the hypothesis is in Ta-Hf (83:17), where an ~0.37 K Tc decrease is observed from 550°C to 850°C, but this trend is non-monotonic (650°C yields Tc = 6.18–6.20 K, slightly above 550°C values in some replicates) and is confined to a single alloy system from a single study, limiting generalizability. Uncontrolled confounds across the full dataset — including deposition method (ALD, sputtering, e-beam evaporation), substrate choice, and film thickness — further prevent attribution of any cross-material Tc variation to deposition temperature.

**Evidence:** 28 samples | 1 author-stated | Materials: Re, Re (with Al/AlOx junction), Al, Ta-Hf (83:17), NbN, Ta

**Supporting:** Within the Ta-Hf (83:17) subset (Yang_2026 Table 2, rows 1–8), the lowest deposition temperatures (550°C) yield the highest Tc values (6.17–6.21 K) and the highest deposition temperatures (850°C) yield the lowest Tc values (5.84–6.00 K), directionally consistent with the hypothesis, though the effect is small and non-monotonic (650°C gives 6.18–6.20 K, slightly above 550°C in some replicates).

**Complicating:** NbN deposited at 400°C achieves Tc = 12.0 K while Re deposited at 900°C achieves only Tc = 1.9 K, directly inverting the predicted cross-material trend; Ta films show essentially flat Tc (4.2–4.4 K) across a 600°C deposition temperature range (22°C to 625°C), demonstrating that deposition temperature has negligible effect on Tc for that material system.

**Alternative explanations:** Material identity (elemental composition, crystal structure, and intrinsic electron-phonon coupling) is the primary determinant of Tc and completely dominates any deposition temperature effect in cross-material comparisons; within a given material, deposition temperature likely acts as a proxy for microstructural variables (grain size, phase purity, disorder, strain) rather than as a direct thermodynamic control on Tc.

**QREM implications:** A general QREM mapping function of the form Tc = f(deposition_temperature) is not supported by this dataset and should not be implemented as a cross-material predictor. For the QREM materials-to-device mapping layer, material identity should be encoded as the primary categorical variable determining Tc, with deposition temperature treated as a secondary, material-specific continuous modifier at most. If a within-material correction is desired, the Ta-Hf (83:17) data provide tentative support for a weak monotonically decreasing function (e.g., a small negative linear slope of roughly −1.2 × 10⁻³ K/°C over 550–850°C), but this should not be generalized to other materials until controlled within-material sweeps are available. For Ta and Al, the current evidence supports a near-zero slope correction, and for NbN and Re, no within-material deposition temperature data are available to parameterize any correction function.

**Per-material recommendation:** Yes, this hypothesis should be re-run per material class rather than cross-material. Ta-Hf (83:17) has the most suitable data for a focused re-analysis (8 samples, controlled deposition temperature sweep, single study) and should be prioritized. Ta has 3 samples across a wide temperature range (22–625°C) and warrants re-analysis to confirm the flat Tc response. Al has 3–4 samples but the confound of transport regime (dirty vs. clean limit) must be addressed before a deposition-temperature effect can be isolated. NbN and Re each have too few samples with deposition temperature variation to support a meaningful within-material re-analysis at this time. Re (with Al/AlOx junction) samples should be excluded from Tc-vs-deposition-temperature analysis as the junction architecture complicates the Tc attribution.

**Questions for reviewer:**

**Suggested follow-up:** The highest-value next experiment is a controlled deposition temperature sweep for NbN or Ta — materials with well-characterized bulk Tc and established quantum computing relevance — using a single sputtering system, fixed substrate (e.g., Si/SiO₂), and fixed film thickness, with paired XRD and four-probe Tc measurements at 5–7 deposition temperatures spanning at least 200°C. ## ✗ No monotonic relationship between sheet resistance and internal quality factor is observed across six NbSe2 resonator samples.
**Hypothesis key:** `Qi_internal_vs_sheet_resistance_Ohm_sq`  
**Finding type:** negative | **Confidence:** 0.15 | **Phase B assessment:** unsupported

Across all six NbSe2 samples with paired Qi and sheet resistance measurements (Zaman_2025), the data are non-monotonic and scattered, providing no support for the hypothesis that proximity to the superconductor-insulator transition (as proxied by Rs) degrades Qi. The highest Qi (~60,000) occurs at an intermediate Rs of 60 Ω/sq (D2), while both the highest-Rs sample (D1, 240 Ω/sq, Qi~8,000) and the lowest-Rs sample (D8, 10 Ω/sq, Qi~7,000) show poor performance. This negative result is itself informative: for NbSe2 van der Waals films in the early exploration regime, sheet resistance does not appear to be the dominant control parameter for microwave loss.

**Detail:** The six NbSe2 samples from Zaman_2025 span Rs values from 10 to 240 Ω/sq and Qi values from ~7,000 to ~60,000, yet no directional trend is discernible. D2 (Rs=60 Ω/sq, dirty limit) outperforms all other samples by a factor of 2.5–8.5×, while D3 (Rs=29 Ω/sq) and D8 (Rs=10 Ω/sq, clean limit) both yield Qi~7,000 — nearly identical despite a 3× difference in Rs. D4 (Rs=25 Ω/sq, Qi~24,000) and D5 (Rs=19 Ω/sq, Qi~12,000) are non-monotonic with each other, with lower Rs yielding lower Qi. The single author-stated correlation seed derives from Böttcher_2025, which studied NbN films fabricated by atomic layer etching — a different material system and fabrication route — and itself reported a null result; its relevance to NbSe2 van der Waals films is therefore limited. The derived-field relationship between Rs and kinetic inductance (Lk ∝ Rs) means Rs is not fully independent of superconducting electrodynamics, but this does not rescue the hypothesis given the non-monotonic scatter observed.

**Evidence:** 6 samples | 1 author-stated | Materials: NbSe2

**Supporting:** D1 (Rs=240 Ω/sq) does show low Qi (~8,000), which is nominally consistent with the hypothesis that high Rs near the SIT degrades Qi; however, this single data point cannot establish a trend and is contradicted by the remaining five samples.

**Complicating:** D2 (Rs=60 Ω/sq) achieves the highest Qi (~60,000) despite having higher sheet resistance than D3, D4, D5, and D8, directly falsifying a monotonic Rs-Qi relationship; additionally, D8 (Rs=10 Ω/sq, clean limit) shows Qi~7,000, demonstrating that low sheet resistance does not guarantee high Qi.

**Alternative explanations:** Crystal quality, stoichiometry, layer number, and substrate-film interface quality likely dominate Qi variation in these van der Waals NbSe2 films, masking any Rs-Qi correlation; surface oxidation and adsorbate chemistry on this air-sensitive material may also contribute independently of Rs to two-level system (TLS) loss and Qi scatter.

**QREM implications:** A QREM materials-to-device mapping function of the form Qi = f(Rs) is not supported by current data and should not be implemented for NbSe2. No monotonic, invertible, or even qualitatively directional mapping can be extracted from these six samples. If a mapping layer is to be built for NbSe2, the primary input variables should instead reflect crystal quality proxies (e.g., RRR, Tc suppression, Raman linewidth) and surface/interface loss metrics rather than sheet resistance. For other material classes (NbN, TiN, Al), the hypothesis remains untested in this corpus and a mapping function cannot be ruled out; however, no data currently exist in this corpus to parameterize such a function. The derived-field relationship Lk ∝ Rs means that if kinetic inductance fraction is used as a QREM input, Rs should not be treated as an independent predictor of Qi to avoid double-counting correlated variables.

**Per-material recommendation:** NbSe2 is the only material with any paired Qi/Rs data in this corpus (6 samples), and while this is the largest single-material cluster available, it is insufficient to resolve the hypothesis — a focused re-analysis of NbSe2 would require at minimum 15–20 samples with controlled layer number and surface preparation to separate Rs from confounds. No other material class (NbN, TiN, Al, Nb) has enough samples with both fields measured to warrant a focused re-analysis at this time. Priority for new data collection should be NbN (given the Böttcher_2025 seed) and TiN (where Rs variation near the SIT is well-documented in the literature), as these would allow cross-material comparison.

**Questions for reviewer:**
- D2 (Rs=60 Ω/sq) achieves Qi~60,000 while D3 (Rs=29 Ω/sq) achieves only ~7,000 — do you have insight into what distinguishes these two samples beyond sheet resistance (e.g., layer count, encapsulation quality, fabrication run)? This outlier is the most important data point in the dataset.
- NbSe2 is highly air-sensitive: were all six samples fabricated and measured under comparable inert-atmosphere or encapsulation conditions, or could differential surface oxidation between samples plausibly account for the ~8× Qi spread observed?
- The hypothesis was seeded by a Böttcher_2025 NbN result — is there a physical reason to expect the Rs-Qi relationship to transfer from NbN (a disordered BCS superconductor) to NbSe2 (a van der Waals CDW/superconductor), or should this seed be deprioritized for NbSe2 analyses?
- Should sheet resistance be replaced or supplemented by residual resistivity ratio (RRR) or normal-state resistivity at a fixed temperature as the disorder proxy for NbSe2, given that Rs conflates layer number and intrinsic disorder in van der Waals films?

**Suggested follow-up:** The most valuable next step is a controlled fabrication series of NbSe2 resonators in which a single variable (e.g., number of layers, or annealing condition) is systematically varied to produce a range of Rs values while holding surface preparation, encapsulation, substrate, and resonator geometry constant — then measuring Qi alongside Raman spectroscopy and AFM to quantify crystal quality independently of Rs. This would allow the Rs-Qi relationship to be tested with confounds controlled, and would directly test whether the D2 outlier reflects a reproducible optimum or a sample-specific anomaly.
