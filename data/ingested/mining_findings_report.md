**Supporting:** The single contrasting data point — Joshi_2026 beta-Ta on sapphire (Tc = 0.7 K, derived_tan_delta = 0.0016) versus Bland_2025 alpha-Ta on Si (Tc = 4.2 K, derived_tan_delta = 0.00095–0.00113) — is directionally consistent with the hypothesis that higher Tc accompanies lower microwave loss.

**Complicating:** The two sample groups differ simultaneously in at least three major confounding variables (Ta crystal phase, substrate material, and transport regime), making it impossible to attribute any tan_delta difference to Tc; furthermore, within-group variation at fixed Tc = 4.2 K (~19% spread across Bland_2025 samples) indicates that Tc alone does not determine tan_delta.

**Alternative explanations:** The most plausible alternative is that Ta crystal phase (alpha vs. beta) is the true underlying variable driving both Tc and tan_delta simultaneously, with substrate-dependent TLS loss and transport-regime-dependent quasiparticle loss as additional independent contributors — none of which require a direct causal link between Tc and microwave loss tangent.

**QREM implications:** A QREM materials-to-device mapping function of the form tan_delta = f(Tc) is not supportable from this corpus. The data do not provide evidence that Tc can serve as a reliable predictor input for microwave loss in a mapping layer: within-material variation at fixed Tc is comparable in magnitude to the cross-material variation attributed to Tc differences, and the dominant confounders (crystal phase, substrate, transport regime) are not controlled. If a mapping function were to be constructed, it would need to encode crystal phase and substrate identity as primary categorical inputs, with Tc as a secondary continuous variable whose independent contribution remains unquantified. Until controlled experiments isolating Tc as an independent variable are available, any QREM mapping that uses Tc as a proxy for tan_delta in Ta films risks conflating phase-selection effects with a spurious Tc dependence.

**Per-material recommendation:** No material class in the current corpus has sufficient samples to warrant a statistically meaningful focused re-analysis of this specific hypothesis. Ta has 4 samples but they are too heavily confounded for isolation of the Tc effect. A focused re-analysis would become warranted for Ta if at least 8–10 samples spanning a continuous range of Tc values (achievable via alloying or annealing) on a single substrate type with matched geometry were added. Nb and NbTiN are absent from this corpus entirely and should be considered for a parallel hypothesis test if those corpora are larger, as they would provide a cross-material check on whether any Tc–tan_delta relationship is Ta-specific or universal.

This determines whether the seeding claim itself is directly testable from existing data.
